# Linguine with Clams, Cherry Tomatoes, and Capers

## Description
Clams and linguine are a classic combination, and this version bumps up the flavor with a handful of fresh cherry tomatoes and briny capers.

## Ingredients
_(for 4 servings)_

- 4 tablespoons unsalted butter, divided
- 1 onion, chopped fine
- 6 cloves garlic, sliced thin
- 1/2 teaspoon red pepper flakes
- 1/2 cup dry white wine or vermouth
- 1/2 teaspoon lemon zest and 2 tablespoons lemon juice
- 1/2 cup chicken broth
- 36 littleneck clams (3 to 4 pounds), scrubbed
- 18 ounces (3 cups) cherry tomatoes, halved
- 3 tablespoons drained capers, rinsed
- 1/2 cup grated Parmesan cheese
- 2 tablespoons minced fresh parsley, divided
- 1 pound linguine
- table salt, for cooking pasta

## Instructions
1. Melt 2 tablespoons butter in a Dutch oven (or brasier) over medium heat. Add onion, garlic, and pepper flakes and cook until softened and lightly browned, 3 to 5 minutes. Stir in wine and lemon zest and simmer until liquid is reduced and syrupy, about 2 minutes. Stir in broth and clams, cover, and increase heat to high. Cook until clams open, 4 to 8 minutes.
2. Using a slotted spoon, transfer clams to a large bowl (discard any unopened clams) and cover to keep warm. Stir tomatoes, capers, and lemon juice into the liquid left in the skillet and simmer over high heat until tomatoes are softened, 4 to 6 minutes. Off heat, stir in Parmesan, 1 tablespoon parsley, and the remaining 2 tablespoons butter; sauce will look a bit watery. Season with salt and pepper to taste, cover, and set aside until pasta is cooked.
3. Meanwhile, bring 4 quarts water to a boil in a large pot for the pasta. Add pasta and 2 teaspoons salt and cook, stirring often, until just shy of al dente. Drain pasta, add to sauce, and simmer over high heat until pasta is al dente and sauce has thickened nicely, 2 to 4 minutes. Transfer to a serving platter (if desired), top with clams, garnish with the remaining 1 tablespoon parsley, and serve.

## Cook's Notes & Tips
Discard any raw clams with unpleasant odors, cracked or broken shells, or open shells that won't close when tapped. Because the saltiness of fresh clams can vary, add less salt than usual to the pasta cooking water. It's crucial to steam the clams right in the sauce so their flavor winds up in the finished dish, and to finish cooking the pasta in the sauce so it absorbs flavor and helps thicken the sauce with its starch. Clams are removed from the sauce once cooked through (to prevent overcooking) and nestled back into the finished dish at the end.
