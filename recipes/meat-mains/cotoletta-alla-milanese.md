# Cotoletta alla Milanese

## Description
Italy's answer to the breaded cutlet: veal pounded thin, coated in breadcrumbs, and pan-fried in clarified butter (never oil, per tradition) until golden and crisp outside while tender inside. A Milanese classic dating back to at least the 12th century. [Source](https://www.gamintraveler.com/2025/07/27/how-to-make-an-authentic-cotoletta-alla-milanese/)

## Ingredients
_(for 4 servings)_

- 4 veal cutlets (about 6 ounces each), bone-in, pounded to about 1/4 inch thickness
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste
- 1/2 cup all-purpose flour
- 2 large eggs, beaten
- 1 cup breadcrumbs
- 1/2 cup grated Parmesan cheese (optional, for added flavor)
- Olive oil or clarified butter, for frying
- Lemon wedges, for serving

## Instructions
1. If not already done by your butcher, pound the veal cutlets to an even thickness of about 1/4 inch. Season each cutlet with salt and pepper on both sides.
2. Set up a breading station with three shallow dishes: one with flour, one with beaten eggs, and one with breadcrumbs mixed with grated Parmesan (if using).
3. Dredge each cutlet in flour, shaking off excess. Dip in the beaten eggs, letting excess drip off. Coat evenly with the breadcrumb mixture, pressing gently to adhere.
4. Heat a generous amount of olive oil or clarified butter in a large skillet over medium-high heat — enough to come halfway up the sides of the cutlets.
5. Once the oil is hot, add the cutlets (in batches if needed to avoid overcrowding) and fry for about 3 to 4 minutes on each side, or until golden brown and crispy. Transfer to a paper towel-lined plate to drain.
6. Serve hot with lemon wedges, squeezing lemon over the cutlet just before eating.

## Cook's Notes & Tips
- Pounding cutlets to an even thickness ensures they cook evenly.
- Make sure the oil is hot enough before frying — if too cool, the breading absorbs oil and turns soggy.
- Fry in batches so the oil temperature doesn't drop too much.
- Parmesan in the breadcrumbs adds flavor depth but can be omitted for a more traditional version.
- Traditionalists insist on bone-in veal, shallow-fried in clarified butter (not deep-fried in oil); many modern versions substitute pork or chicken.
- Source estimates approximately 400–600 calories per serving, depending on cutlet size and frying fat used.
