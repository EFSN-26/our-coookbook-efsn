# Sugo della Nonna: Italian Sunday Tomato Sauce

## Description
The slow-cooked tomato sauce ("sugo della nonna") many Italian households make on Sundays — tomatoes, olive oil, garlic and onion, and fresh basil, simmered low and slow for hours until deep and rustic. This version is a meatless, Southern Italian-style base with several traditional optional add-ins (carrot, Parmesan rind, or a meat bone/sausage). [Source](https://www.gamintraveler.com/2026/07/19/sunday-tomato-sauce-recipe-italian-grandmothers/)

## Ingredients
_(for 4 to 6 servings)_

- 2 tablespoons extra virgin olive oil
- 1 small yellow onion, finely chopped
- 3 cloves garlic, peeled and smashed (not minced)
- 2 (28 oz / 800g) cans whole peeled San Marzano tomatoes, crushed by hand
- 1 tablespoon tomato paste
- 1 teaspoon sugar (optional, for acidity balance)
- 8-10 fresh basil leaves, torn
- salt, to taste
- black pepper, to taste
- 1 small carrot, grated (optional, adds natural sweetness)
- 1 Parmesan rind, added while simmering (optional)
- 1 small meat bone (beef or pork) or sausage, for added depth (optional)

## Instructions
1. **Crush the tomatoes:** Open the cans and pour the tomatoes into a large bowl. Crush by hand for a rustic texture. Do not discard the juice.
2. **Prep aromatics:** Finely chop the onion. Peel and smash the garlic cloves (do not mince).
3. **Prep optional ingredients:** Rinse and tear the basil leaves by hand. If using meat, pat it dry. If using carrot, grate it now.
4. **Sauté aromatics:** In a large heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven over medium-low heat, warm the olive oil. Add the chopped onion and stir gently for 5-7 minutes until translucent (do not brown). Add the garlic and stir for another 1-2 minutes until fragrant.
5. **Add tomato paste:** Stir in the tomato paste and cook gently for 1-2 minutes to deepen the flavor.
6. **Add tomatoes:** Pour in the hand-crushed tomatoes and all their juices. Stir to combine.
7. **Season:** Add salt, a few grinds of black pepper, and sugar if needed. If using, add the grated carrot and the Parmesan rind or meat bone now.
8. **Simmer:** Reduce the heat to low and let the sauce simmer uncovered for at least 1.5 to 2 hours, stirring every 20 minutes to prevent sticking. The longer it simmers, the deeper the flavor.
9. **Add basil:** In the last 15-20 minutes of simmering, stir in the torn basil leaves.
10. **Taste and adjust:** Remove the garlic cloves, Parmesan rind, or meat if used. Taste for salt, sweetness, and acidity.
11. **Optional — blend:** For a smoother sauce, partially blend with a hand blender. Many Italians leave it rustic instead.

## Cook's Notes & Tips
- Serve over al dente pasta (rigatoni, bucatini, or spaghetti are suggested), topped with freshly grated Parmigiano-Reggiano or Pecorino Romano, alongside crusty bread and a glass of red wine. Also works in lasagna, stuffed shells, or eggplant parmigiana.
- Leftovers keep refrigerated up to 5 days or frozen up to 3 months, and the source notes the flavor deepens by the next day.
- Traditionally the meats cooked in the sauce (if used) are served as a separate second course rather than mixed into the pasta.
- Variations by region and family are common: some cooks use only garlic, others only onion, or both (this version uses both for a balanced Southern-style base).
- **A note on timing in the source:** the step-by-step recipe itself calls for simmering "at least 1.5 to 2 hours," but a separate section of the same article describes traditional Sunday sauce as cooking for "three to six hours, sometimes longer." Both figures come from the same source article; this file records the recipe's own stated 1.5-2 hour minimum as the cook time, but a longer simmer (per the source's own note on tradition) will deepen the flavor further if you have the time.
