# One Dough, Four Breads Europeans Actually Eat On Weeknights

## Description
A single medium-hydration base dough — bread flour, water, salt, and a small amount of instant yeast — that divides into four different European weeknight breads: skillet flatbreads, a sheet-pan focaccia, a quick pizza, and warm stuffed rolls. The base batch makes about 1.2 kg of dough, yielding roughly 8–12 servings depending on which shapes you make, enough for either two pizzas plus two skillet flatbreads, or one tray focaccia plus six to eight stuffed rolls. There are two schedules: a same-day plan (about 2 hours end to end) and an overnight cold-ferment plan (12–24 hours) that builds more flavor. [Source](https://www.gamintraveler.com/2026/07/12/one-dough-four-breads-europeans-actually-eat-on-weeknights/)

## Ingredients
_(for the master dough — about 1.2 kg total, ~10 servings across shapes; baker's percentages are as stated by the source, alongside the source's own base weights)_

**Master dough**
- 750 g bread flour or strong white flour (100 percent)
- 488 g water, room temperature for same-day / cool for overnight (65 percent hydration)
- 15 g fine sea salt (2 percent)
- 3.5 g instant yeast (0.5 percent) for the same-day method, OR 1.5 g instant yeast (0.2 percent) for the overnight method
- extra virgin olive oil, for pans and finishing (amount not specified by source)
- optional: sugar or honey, 1 percent of flour weight, for faster browning (amount not specified by source)

**Bread One — Skillet Flatbreads (4 flatbreads)**
- 4 pieces master dough, 150–180 g each
- 2 tablespoons olive oil
- fine salt, to taste
- optional: sesame seeds, za'atar, or dried oregano

**Bread Two — Sheet Pan Focaccia (1 pan, 8–10 pieces)**
- all 1.2 kg master dough (or 900 g for a thinner tray)
- 60 mL olive oil, for pan and top
- 10 g flaky salt
- optional toppings: cherry tomatoes, sliced onion, rosemary, olives

**Bread Three — Quick Pizza (2 pizzas, 2–3 people each)**
- 2 pieces master dough, 300–350 g each
- 1 cup tomato sauce base, per pizza (source references its own "Five-Ingredient Tomato Base")
- 150–200 g mozzarella per pizza, well drained, sliced or grated
- olive oil and salt, to taste
- optional: basil leaves, sliced mushrooms, olives, anchovies

**Bread Four — Warm Stuffed Rolls (6–8 rolls)**
- 6–8 pieces master dough, 140–170 g each
- choice of filling:
  - spinach and feta: 250 g cooked, squeezed spinach + 120 g crumbled feta + black pepper + pinch of nutmeg
  - tomato and mozzarella: 6 tablespoons thick tomato sauce + 120 g diced mozzarella + pinch of oregano
  - ham and cheese: 150 g diced ham + 120 g grated cheese + black pepper
  - tuna and olive: 1 tin tuna in olive oil, drained and flaked + 8 chopped olives + squeeze of lemon

## Instructions

**Master dough — Same-Day Method (about 2 hours total, 20 minutes active)**
1. Mix: in a large bowl, whisk the flour, salt, and yeast. Add the room-temperature water and stir with a spatula until no dry flour remains — the dough will look shaggy. Rest 10 minutes.
2. Fold: with wet fingers, pick up one edge of the dough, stretch it, and fold it over the center. Rotate the bowl a quarter turn and repeat for 8–10 folds total. Rest 10 minutes, then repeat the folding once more. The dough should look smoother and hold its shape.
3. Rise: cover and rest 45–60 minutes at warm room temperature until slightly puffy.
4. Divide: scrape onto a lightly oiled counter and divide into the shapes you plan to cook. For pizza, cut 2 pieces of 350 g. For flatbreads or rolls, cut 4–6 pieces of 150–200 g. Shape into tight balls and rest 20 minutes under a towel.
5. Proceed to the specific bread method below.

**Master dough — Overnight Method (12–24 hours, 15 minutes active on each of two days)**
1. Mix the flour, salt, and yeast; add cool water; mix until no dry flour remains.
2. Do one round of folds as in the same-day method, until the dough becomes smoother.
3. Cover and refrigerate 12–24 hours — the dough rises slowly and develops flavor.
4. Two hours before baking, bring the bowl to room temperature, then divide and shape as in the same-day method.

**Bread One — Skillet Flatbreads (15 minutes active, 25 minutes total)**
1. Preheat a skillet over medium-high heat until a drop of water sizzles.
2. Roll each dough ball on a lightly oiled counter into an 18–20 cm round, 4–5 mm thick. Keep remaining balls covered.
3. Brush the skillet with a film of olive oil. Cook one round 90 seconds until bubbles rise and the bottom has golden patches, then flip and cook 60–90 seconds more.
4. Brush lightly with olive oil, sprinkle with salt and herbs, and repeat with remaining rounds, adjusting heat so the breads color without smoking.

**Bread Two — Sheet Pan Focaccia (20 minutes active, 2 hours same-day / 16 hours overnight)**
1. Oil a sheet pan with 3 tablespoons olive oil. Transfer the dough to the pan and gently stretch to fill about two-thirds of the tray. Rest 15 minutes.
2. Stretch to the corners with oiled fingers. Rest 15 minutes.
3. Dimple deeply with fingertips, drizzle 2 tablespoons olive oil over the surface, scatter toppings (tomatoes, onions, olives, rosemary), and sprinkle half the flaky salt.
4. Proof 30–40 minutes until puffy and jiggly.
5. Bake at 230°C for 18–22 minutes until deep golden with crisp edges.
6. Finish with the remaining olive oil and flaky salt. Cool 10 minutes, then cut.
7. For the overnight version: after the cold rest, spread the cold dough in the oiled pan, cover, and let warm 60 minutes, then proceed from step 2.

**Bread Three — Quick Pizza (25 minutes active, 45–60 minutes total including preheat)**
1. Preheat the oven to 250°C with a plain, inverted sheet pan on the middle rack (the hot metal mimics a pizza stone).
2. Shape each dough ball on parchment, pressing and stretching to a 28–30 cm round with a slightly thicker edge. Do not use a rolling pin.
3. Top with a thin layer of tomato sauce, scattered cheese, a drizzle of olive oil, a pinch of salt, and toppings used sparingly.
4. Slide the parchment onto the preheated pan and bake 8–11 minutes until the edge is browned and the cheese bubbles.
5. Finish with fresh basil and a thread of olive oil. Repeat for the second pizza.

**Bread Four — Warm Stuffed Rolls (30 minutes active, 1 hour 30 minutes same-day / 14 hours overnight)**
1. Divide dough into 6–8 pieces of 140–170 g each. Rest 10 minutes.
2. Flatten each piece into a 14 cm round, keeping the edges slightly thinner than the center.
3. Fill the center with 2–3 tablespoons of chosen filling. Fold the edges over the center, pinch to seal, then flip seam-side down and tighten into a ball with cupped hands.
4. Proof on parchment 30 minutes until puffy.
5. Bake at 210°C for 14–18 minutes until golden. Brush with a little olive oil for shine. Cool 10 minutes before biting to avoid molten centers.

## Cook's Notes & Tips
- The 65 percent hydration keeps the dough soft enough for focaccia and pizza yet firm enough to roll easily; the 2 percent salt seasons it without slowing yeast activity too much.
- Short folds (rather than long kneading) build gluten structure.
- Temperature-sensitive timing: in a cold kitchen, use slightly warmer water and extend rests; in a hot kitchen, use cool water and shorten rests.
- Troubleshooting per source: dough that tears when stretching needs more rest (cover, wait 10 minutes, retry); a flat pizza edge usually means underproofed or over-pressed dough — rest shaped balls 20–30 minutes and never use a rolling pin; dense focaccia usually means not enough oil under the dough or underproofing — the tray should jiggle "like a mattress" when tapped when ready; pale bottoms mean the oven needs a longer preheat or an inverted tray underneath for extra bottom heat; stuffed rolls leak if the filling is too wet or the seal is weak — squeeze spinach dry and pinch seams hard, baking seam-down.
- Storage: dough keeps up to 2 hours at room temperature after the bulk rise (same-day) or up to 24 hours refrigerated (overnight). Baked breads keep 1 day at room temperature (bagged once cool) or up to 3 months frozen, wrapped well.
- Reheating: flatbreads in a dry skillet, 30–45 seconds per side; focaccia and rolls in the oven at 180°C for 6–8 minutes; pizza in the oven at 220°C for 5–7 minutes on a hot tray. A splash of water on the tray helps steam the crumb back to life.
- Flour swap: replace 15–20 percent of the white flour with whole wheat or spelt for a nuttier crumb, and increase water by 10–15 g if the dough feels stiff.
- Cost estimate given by the source (mid-range European supermarket, not verified independently): roughly €1.70–€2.90 for the base dough, yielding 8–12 servings depending on shape — about €0.60–€1.50 per serving before toppings/fillings.
- Per the source, this specific dough does not work as a gluten-free swap; a gluten-free yeasted-dough blend would need its own hydration and proof times from the blend maker.
