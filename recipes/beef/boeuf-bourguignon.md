# Boeuf Bourguignon

## Description
A classic French wine-braised beef stew with bacon, glazed pearl onions and browned mushrooms, built around a slow six-hour timeline so the collagen in the beef fully melts into a silky, spoon-coating sauce. [Source](https://www.gamintraveler.com/2025/12/13/why-this-french-stew-takes-6-hours-and-european-grandmothers-refuse-to-rush-it-a-recipe-guide/)

## Ingredients
_(for 6 servings)_

**Beef and basics**
- 1.5 kg beef chuck or shin, cut in 5 cm cubes (about 3.3 lb)
- 150 g smoked bacon or slab pancetta, cut in lardons (about 5 oz)
- 2 tablespoons tomato paste
- 2 medium onions, diced
- 2 carrots, sliced thick
- 4 garlic cloves, smashed
- 2 tablespoons flour
- 750 ml red wine, Burgundy style if available, or any honest dry red (about 3 cups)
- 750 ml beef stock or water (about 3 cups)
- 2 bay leaves, 6 thyme sprigs, 6 parsley stems
- salt and black pepper, to taste
- 2 tablespoons olive oil or neutral oil, plus bacon fat

**Garnish**
- 300 g pearl onions or small shallots, peeled (about 2 cups)
- 300 g mushrooms, halved or quartered (about 10 oz)
- 25 g butter, for onions
- 25 g butter, for mushrooms
- pinch of sugar, for glazing onions
- parsley, to finish

**Optional finish**
- 1 tablespoon softened butter mashed with 1 tablespoon flour, for emergency thickening

## Instructions
1. Pat the beef dry. Toss with 2.5 teaspoons salt and 1 teaspoon black pepper. If possible, do this the night before and refrigerate; otherwise do it now and let it sit while you chop. Tie the bay, thyme and parsley stems into a bundle if you want to fish them out easily later.
2. Put a heavy pot (Dutch oven, 4.5 to 6 quarts) over medium heat. Add the bacon and cook until the fat renders and the pieces crisp. Remove bacon to a plate, leaving the fat in the pot.
3. Raise the heat a notch. Add half the beef in one layer. Brown without moving for 3 to 4 minutes, then turn and brown another side. Remove to the plate with the bacon and repeat with the remaining beef.
4. Lower heat to medium. Add the onions and carrots to the pot, with a spoon of oil if the pot looks dry. Scrape the bottom. Cook until glossy and starting to color, 6 to 8 minutes. Add the garlic for 30 seconds. Stir in the tomato paste and cook until brick red, about 90 seconds.
5. Sprinkle the flour over the vegetables and stir for 60 seconds.
6. Pour in the wine. Scrape the bottom. Bring to a simmer and reduce by about a third, 6 to 8 minutes.
7. Return the beef and bacon to the pot. Add stock or water to barely cover. Tuck in the herb bundle. Bring just to a simmer and skim any foam.
8. Cover with a lid slightly ajar (or a parchment circle under the lid). Adjust heat to the laziest simmer and cook 2.5 hours, stirring a couple of times.
9. Uncover and simmer 30 minutes to concentrate the sauce. Fish out the herb bundle. Test a piece of beef — a fork should slide in with little resistance; if it fights, give it 20 more minutes.
10. Meanwhile, make the garnish: in a skillet, melt 25 g butter with a spoon of water and a small pinch of sugar. Add the pearl onions with a pinch of salt. Cover and cook on medium-low until tender and glazed, 12 to 15 minutes, shaking occasionally.
11. In the same or a second skillet, melt 25 g butter. Add the mushrooms in a single layer. Brown without fussing for 4 to 5 minutes, then stir once and finish another 3 minutes. Salt at the end.
12. Stir the onions and mushrooms into the stew. Taste the sauce. If thin, simmer uncovered 10 to 15 minutes. If it needs more body, whisk in the optional butter-flour paste in small dots and simmer 2 to 3 minutes.
13. Either turn off the heat and let the pot rest 30 to 45 minutes before serving, or cool, refrigerate, and reheat gently the next day. Finish with parsley.

## Cook's Notes & Tips
- The source frames the long cook time as essential: collagen in tough, well-marbled cuts (chuck, shoulder, beef shin, paleron) only melts into tenderizing gelatin with gentle heat held over hours — a rolling boil shreds the meat and clouds the sauce instead.
- Common fixes from the source: watery sauce means under-reduced (simmer uncovered longer); tough meat means it needs more low simmering time, not more heat; broken/grainy mushrooms mean the pan was salted too early or crowded (brown in batches, salt at the end); greasy top can be lifted off in one piece after chilling overnight; a too-winey sauce needs longer reduction or a knob of butter stirred in at the end.
- Two weeknight shortcuts given by the source: (1) brown beef and vegetables in a pressure cooker, add wine and stock, cook on high pressure 40 minutes with a 15-minute natural release, then finish reducing and add the garnish on the stovetop; (2) a simpler "daube style" version that skips the pearl onions and mushrooms in favor of a strip of orange peel and a few black olives added in the last 20 minutes.
- Suggested serving sides: buttered smashed potatoes, egg noodles, creamy polenta, or crusty bread, plus a simply dressed green salad to cut the richness.
- Leftover ideas from the source: shepherd's pie with shredded beef and mashed potato topping; toss the reduced sauce with egg noodles (tagliatelle night); or spoon warmed stew over garlic-rubbed grilled bread.
- Freezes well in flat bags, per the source, since gelatin holds up through freezing and reheating.
- The source estimates a home-cooked batch (serving 6, with leftovers) costs roughly €35–45 total in Spain/France, versus €18–28 for a single restaurant portion — provided as the source's own cost estimate, not independently verified.
