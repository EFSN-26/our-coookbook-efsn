# Spanish Oxtail Stew (Rabo de Toro)

## Description
A slow-braised Spanish oxtail stew known as rabo de toro, a dish tied to long braises, red wine, and unhurried Sunday cooking in Andalusia and beyond. The connective-tissue-rich cut turns silky and the sauce naturally thick and glossy after hours of gentle simmering — no cream or flour needed to "rescue" it.

## Ingredients
_(for 6 servings)_

- 1.6 kg (about 3.5 lb) oxtail, cut into rounds
- 2 tsp salt, plus more to taste
- 1 tsp black pepper
- 2 tbsp (about 16 g) flour, optional
- 3 tbsp (45 ml) olive oil
- 2 large onions (about 400 g), diced
- 2 carrots (about 200 g), diced
- 2 celery sticks (about 120 g), diced
- 6 to 8 cloves garlic, smashed and chopped
- 2 tbsp (30 g) tomato paste
- 1 tsp paprika (sweet, or half sweet and half spicy)
- 500 ml (2 cups) red wine
- 750 ml (3 cups) beef stock, plus extra splash if needed
- 2 bay leaves
- 1 tsp dried thyme (or 3 fresh sprigs)
- 6 whole black peppercorns, optional
- 5 g dark chocolate (1 small piece), optional, added at the end for gloss, not sweetness

## Instructions
1. **Season and prep the oxtail.** Pat the pieces dry. Season with salt and pepper. If using flour, dust lightly — this is just to help browning, not a full breading.
2. **Brown in batches.** Heat olive oil in a heavy pot over medium-high. Brown the oxtail in batches, 3 to 4 minutes per side, without crowding the pot. Move browned pieces to a plate.
3. **Build the base.** Lower heat to medium. Add the onion, carrot, and celery with a pinch of salt. Cook 10 minutes until softened. Add the garlic and cook 30 seconds.
4. **Tomato and paprika.** Stir in the tomato paste and cook 1 minute. Add the paprika and stir quickly so it doesn't burn.
5. **Deglaze with wine.** Pour in the red wine and scrape the bottom of the pot. Simmer 8 to 10 minutes to reduce slightly.
6. **Braise.** Return the oxtail to the pot. Add the stock, bay leaves, thyme, and peppercorns if using. The liquid should come about halfway up the meat. Bring to a gentle simmer.
7. **Cook low and slow.** Cover and simmer on low for 3 hours, turning the pieces once or twice — or move to the oven at 160°C (320°F) for the same time. You want a lazy bubble, not a boil.
8. **Finish the sauce.** Remove the lid for the last 20 to 30 minutes to thicken. Taste and adjust salt. If using the dark chocolate, add it now and stir until melted.
9. **Rest.** Turn off the heat and let it rest 15 minutes before serving.

## Cook's Notes & Tips
- **For a smoother, restaurant-style sauce (optional):** lift the oxtail pieces out and keep warm. Strain the sauce into a bowl, pressing the vegetables to extract their flavor. For an even thicker, glossier sauce, blend the strained vegetables with a little of the liquid, then return to the pot and simmer uncovered for 10 minutes.
- Alternatively, the source's own grandmother simply reduces the sauce and leaves the vegetables in, rather than straining/blending.
- Use a wine you'd actually drink — not expensive, just not bad, since a poor wine makes a poor sauce.
- Don't rush the final reduction: boiling it hard gives sharpness, while simmering gives roundness.
- This dish improves overnight as the sauce thickens and flavors settle — cooking it a day ahead is recommended, not just a convenience.
- **Common mistakes to avoid (per the source):** skipping the browning step (loses depth); cooking too hot/boiling instead of a gentle simmer (makes the sauce harsh and meat tough); not reducing the sauce enough at the end; under-salting at the finish; expecting a high meat-to-bone ratio (oxtail is bone-heavy by nature — a half-and-half approach with 500-700g of beef shank, chuck, or cheek alongside the oxtail stretches the servings if desired); serving it without something to hold the sauce (potatoes, bread, rice); and judging it right out of the pot rather than letting it rest/rewarming the next day.
- **Storage:** keeps 4 days in the fridge; freezes (meat and sauce together) for up to 3 months.
- **Serving suggestion:** potatoes, bread, or rice.
- **Buying tip:** ask the butcher for oxtail cut into thick rounds rather than the thinnest end pieces, which are mostly bone; thicker pieces brown better and hold up through the long braise.
