# Tagliatelle al Ragù

## Description
An authentic Bolognese-style ragù from Emilia-Romagna, simmered for hours with a soffritto of onion, carrot and celery, white wine, milk, and just a touch of tomato, then tossed with fresh tagliatelle. The source is emphatic that this is not the same thing as "spaghetti Bolognese" — true ragù alla Bolognese uses more meat than tomato and is always paired with a wide, ribboned fresh egg pasta rather than dried spaghetti.

## Ingredients
_(for 4 servings)_

### For the Ragù
- 2 tablespoons olive oil
- 1 tablespoon unsalted butter
- 1 onion, finely chopped
- 1 carrot, finely chopped
- 1 celery stalk, finely chopped
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 8 oz (225 g) ground beef
- 8 oz (225 g) ground pork (or a mix of veal and pork)
- 1/2 cup dry white wine
- 1 cup whole milk
- 1 cup crushed tomatoes (or passata)
- 1/4 cup tomato paste
- 1 cup beef or chicken broth
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste
- Freshly grated nutmeg, a pinch (optional)

### For the Pasta
- 12 oz (350 g) tagliatelle pasta (preferably fresh)
- Salt, for the pasta water
- Freshly grated Parmesan cheese, for serving

## Instructions
1. In a large, heavy-bottomed pot, heat the olive oil and butter over medium heat. Add the chopped onion, carrot, and celery. Sauté about 10 minutes, until softened and golden. Add the minced garlic and cook 1 more minute.
2. Add the ground beef and ground pork. Cook, breaking up the meat with a wooden spoon, until browned all over, about 10 minutes. Season with salt and pepper.
3. Pour in the white wine and cook a few minutes, until mostly evaporated.
4. Stir in the milk and let it simmer gently until absorbed, about 10 minutes.
5. Add the crushed tomatoes, tomato paste, and broth. Bring to a simmer, then reduce heat to low.
6. Let the sauce cook slowly on low heat for at least 2 hours, preferably 3 to 4 hours, stirring occasionally. If it becomes too thick, add a bit more broth or water. The sauce should be thick and rich.
7. In a large pot of boiling salted water, cook the tagliatelle according to package instructions until al dente — fresh tagliatelle takes about 2-3 minutes, dried tagliatelle longer. Reserve a cup of pasta water, then drain and return the pasta to the pot.
8. Add a few ladles of the ragù to the pasta, tossing to coat evenly. Add a splash of reserved pasta water if needed to loosen the sauce.
9. Divide the pasta among serving plates. Top with more ragù and freshly grated Parmesan cheese. Serve immediately.

## Cook's Notes & Tips
- The key to a great ragù is slow cooking — simmering for several hours develops the deep, rich flavor.
- Tagliatelle is traditional, but pappardelle, fettuccine, or even rigatoni can substitute if tagliatelle isn't available.
- A beef-and-pork combination is common; veal or added pancetta are also traditional variations.
- The ragù can be made ahead — it actually tastes better the next day. Refrigerate up to 3 days, or freeze up to 3 months.
- A light grating of fresh nutmeg adds subtle warmth to the sauce.
- Per the source, authentic ragù alla Bolognese is deeply savory and creamy (built on soffritto, white wine, milk, and only a touch of tomato) rather than acidic/tomato-heavy — versions overloaded with tomato sauce are described as closer to southern Italian or Italian-American styles.
- Approximate calories: 600-750 per serving, based on 4 servings (pasta ~200-250 cal, ragù ~400-500 cal, plus ~50-70 cal per tablespoon of Parmesan), per the source.
- Serving suggestion: a light antipasto of cured meats, Parmigiano-Reggiano and olives as a starter, a robust Sangiovese or Lambrusco alongside, and biscotti dipped in Vin Santo for dessert, for a full regional-style meal.
