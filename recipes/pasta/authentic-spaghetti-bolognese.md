# Authentic Spaghetti Bolognese

## Description
The authentic Italian version of Spaghetti Bolognese, known as Ragù alla Bolognese, is a slow-simmered, deeply flavorful meat sauce with roots in the Emilia-Romagna region of Italy, particularly Bologna. Unlike tomato-heavy versions, true Bolognese is built on a foundation of finely chopped onions, carrots, and celery sautéed in olive oil, combined with ground meat, a splash of wine, a touch of milk, and only a modest amount of tomato.

## Ingredients
_(for 4 servings)_

- 400 g spaghetti
- 500 g ground beef (or a mix of beef and pork)
- 1 large onion
- 2 cloves of garlic
- 2-3 carrots, finely chopped
- 800 g canned chopped tomatoes or tomato sauce
- 2 tablespoons tomato paste
- 1 cup red wine
- 1 cup beef or vegetable broth
- 1 teaspoon dried oregano
- 1 teaspoon dried basil
- Olive oil
- Salt and pepper, to taste
- Fresh basil, for serving
- Grated cheese, for serving

## Instructions
1. **Prepare the sauce:** Heat olive oil in a pan, then add the chopped onion and carrot. Cook until the vegetables are softened, about 5-7 minutes.
2. Add the minced garlic and the meat, breaking it up with a wooden spoon. Cook until the meat is browned and cooked.
3. Pour in the red wine and let it reduce slightly (optional — the recipe works without it too). Add the tomatoes with tomato paste, oregano, and basil, and season with salt and pepper.
4. Add the broth, bring the sauce to a simmer, and let it cook uncovered for about 30 minutes to an hour. The longer it cooks, the better the flavors will meld.
5. **Cook the pasta:** Boil water, add salt and a little oil, then add the spaghetti. Cook for around 8-10 minutes based on package instructions; taste before draining to ensure al dente.
6. **Mix pasta and sauce:** Once the spaghetti is cooked, drain and mix with the sauce, or serve separately if preferred.
7. **Serve:** Serve the spaghetti bolognese hot, with fresh basil and grated Parmesan cheese on top.

## Cook's Notes & Tips
- Let the sauce simmer on low heat for a couple of hours for a richer flavor; the sauce can also be made the day before for more consistent flavor.
- Red wine adds a better taste to the sauce, but if unavailable, the sauce still works with just meat or vegetable broth.
- For the meat, turkey, chicken, pork, beef, or plant-based substitutes can be used — the author's favorite is pork and beef combined, or turkey and chicken combined.
- Authentic Italian ragù is traditionally paired with tagliatelle rather than spaghetti, and uses only a modest amount of tomato rather than a tomato-heavy sauce.
