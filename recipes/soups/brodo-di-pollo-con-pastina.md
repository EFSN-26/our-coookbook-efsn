# Brodo di Pollo con Pastina (Italian Chicken Broth with Pastina)

## Description
There is no question in an Italian household about what to do when someone gets sick: you make brodo. Not broth from a box, not a trendy bone broth — real, homemade brodo di pollo (chicken broth) made with care, simplicity, and time. The most classic version, Brodo di Pollo con Pastina, is chicken broth with tiny pasta: nothing more, nothing less.

## Ingredients
_(for 4-6 servings)_

**For the broth:**
- 1 whole chicken (around 3.5 to 4 lbs), preferably organic
- 2 large carrots, peeled and cut into chunks
- 2 celery stalks, including leaves, cut into chunks
- 1 large yellow onion, peeled and halved
- 1 bay leaf
- A few black peppercorns (optional)
- Salt, to taste
- Water to cover (about 3.5 to 4 quarts)

**For the soup:**
- 1 cup pastina (tiny pasta such as stelline, ditalini, or acini di pepe)
- Grated Parmigiano-Reggiano cheese, for serving
- Optional: a drizzle of good-quality olive oil

## Instructions
1. **Rinse the chicken.** Remove any giblets, rinse the bird under cold water, and pat it dry with a paper towel.
2. **Prepare the vegetables.** Peel and cut the carrots, celery, and onion.
3. **Fill the pot.** Place the whole chicken in a large stockpot (at least 6-quart capacity). Add the vegetables, bay leaf, and peppercorns if using. Cover with cold water by at least 2 inches.
4. **Start cold and heat slowly.** Bring the pot to a gentle simmer over medium heat. As it begins to warm, skim off any foam or impurities that rise to the surface. Do not boil — a gentle simmer keeps the broth clear and golden.
5. **Let it simmer.** Lower the heat to maintain a gentle simmer. Partially cover the pot with a lid and let the broth cook for 2 to 3 hours, until the chicken is falling apart and the vegetables are soft.
6. **Strain the broth.** Remove the chicken and vegetables with a slotted spoon. Strain the broth through a fine mesh strainer or cheesecloth into another pot or bowl. Taste and add salt only after the broth has reduced and settled. Optionally, shred some of the chicken meat and reserve it to add back into the soup.
7. **Cook the pastina.** Bring a small pot of salted water to a boil, add the pastina, and cook according to package directions. Drain and set aside (cooking the pasta separately keeps the broth clear).
8. **Warm the broth.** Bring the strained broth to a gentle simmer again. Add a handful of the cooked pastina, and shredded chicken if desired.
9. **Serve immediately.** Ladle into warm bowls. Top with freshly grated Parmigiano-Reggiano. Some add a few drops of olive oil, or crack a raw egg into the hot broth and stir until it cooks gently as a variation.

## Cook's Notes & Tips
- Some cooks use only the hen; some add a few beef bones for richness. The most classic sick-day version uses just chicken.
- Traditionally served hot but not scalding, in wide shallow bowls, with simple bread or grissini on the side, sometimes with only a spoonful or two of pasta for children or the elderly.
- Traditionally uses small pasta shapes such as stelline (tiny stars) or orzo.
- A drizzle of good olive oil can enhance both flavor and nutrition.
